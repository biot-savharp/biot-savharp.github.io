Praised by Grammy award winner Roger Linn for its

"beautiful tones"

the Biot-Savharp is a table-top harp with 37 steel strings spanning 3 octaves (C3-C6), actuated by electromagnets which are placed above the strings and enabled/disabled at the frequency of the string. The UI, matching the instrument’s discrete nature, consists of a piano-like keyboard. Two switches, conveniently placed, control two “drone” tracks to which the player can add notes by pressing down the keys while flipping the switch—similar to the middle sostenuto pedal of a grand piano. Thus, at no point does anything touch the strings—the energy from the electromagnet is sufficient to make them vibrate.

The underlying physics of this instrument are simple but, according to our research, relatively unexplored in the domain of new musical instruments. In its sustained, resonant, ethereal sound, we see a potential for use in classical, contemporary, or film music, as a polyphonic instrument with a very particular sonority, clear and entrancing.

The Biot-Savharp was awarded "Most Promising Instrument Inventor" at the 2019 Margaret Guthman New Musical Instrument Competition, which Wired magazine calls an "X-Prize for music" and contestants liken to a TED Conference for new musical instrument designers. Receiving praise from the internationally-renowned judges Roger Linn, Pamela Z, and Ge Wang, the Biot-Savharp has captured the attention of the press with its innovative technology and entrancing sound.
